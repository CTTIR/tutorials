Regression & Modelling

Linear, generalised linear, mixed, and additive models, with full attention to diagnostics, interpretation, and reporting

Regression is the general-purpose framework of applied statistics: a way to relate an outcome to one or more predictors while accounting for the structure of the data. This area covers the full regression family, from ordinary least squares through generalised linear models, additive models, mixed-effects models for clustered data, and regularised regression for high-dimensional problems.

Each tutorial is organised around a concrete research question. The reader sees not only how to fit the model in R but how to check its assumptions, interpret its coefficients, summarise its predictions with uncertainty, and present the results in a manuscript table.

Coverage

  • Simple and multiple linear regression; interpretation of coefficients, R-squared, and residual standard error
  • Assumption checks: linearity, homoscedasticity, normality of residuals, independence, multicollinearity
  • Diagnostics: residual plots, leverage, Cook’s distance, VIF, added-variable plots
  • Transformations, interactions, polynomial terms, and splines
  • Logistic regression for binary outcomes; odds ratios, marginal effects, ROC/AUC, calibration
  • Poisson, quasi-Poisson, and negative binomial regression for counts; offsets and rate models
  • Ordinal and multinomial logistic regression
  • Generalised additive models (GAMs) with mgcv; smooth terms and tensor product interactions
  • Linear and generalised linear mixed-effects models with lme4, glmmTMB, and nlme
  • Regularisation: ridge, lasso, elastic net with glmnet
  • Model selection: information criteria, cross-validation, and the dangers of stepwise procedures
  • Robust regression, quantile regression, and generalised estimating equations

Tables and figures in every tutorial are publication-ready, built with broom, gtsummary, modelsummary, and ggeffects.
