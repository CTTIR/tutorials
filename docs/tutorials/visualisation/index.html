Data Visualisation

Publication-quality graphics in R with ggplot2, from the grammar of graphics through colour, annotation, layout, and interactivity

A good figure answers a question at a glance; a bad one hides the answer behind decoration. This area teaches the grammar of graphics as implemented in ggplot2, and builds out from there to every common plot type, colour palette choice, annotation technique, and multi-panel composition used in life-science publications.

Every tutorial uses the same dataset-to-figure workflow: read the data, wrangle it into the tidy shape a plotting call expects, choose geoms and aesthetics deliberately, tune scales and themes for legibility, and export at a resolution suitable for submission.

Topics covered

  • The grammar of graphics: data, mappings, geoms, stats, scales, coordinates, facets, themes
  • Univariate plots: histograms, density plots, ECDFs, boxplots, violin plots, dotplots, raincloud plots
  • Bivariate plots: scatter plots with regression lines, 2D density, hexbin, contour
  • Categorical plots: bar charts, stacked and dodged bars, Cleveland dot plots, heatmaps
  • Time-series plots: line plots, ribbon bands for uncertainty, calendar heatmaps
  • Statistical plots: forest plots, funnel plots, Bland-Altman plots, ROC curves, survival curves
  • Multi-panel layouts: facet_wrap, facet_grid, and the patchwork package for arbitrary compositions
  • Colour theory: perceptually uniform palettes (viridis), colour-blind-safe choices, diverging scales
  • Typography, annotation, labelling, and manuscript-ready export (ggsave, size and DPI conventions)
  • Interactive graphics with plotly, ggiraph, and htmlwidgets for HTML reports

The emphasis throughout is on figures that stand alone: legible axes, informative legends, captions that tell the reader what to see.
