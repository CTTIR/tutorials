Statistical Foundations

The conceptual bedrock of applied statistics: measurement, populations, sampling, estimation theory, and likelihood

Before any specific test or model, an analyst needs a firm grasp of the vocabulary and logic that underlies every statistical procedure. This area walks through what it means to measure a phenomenon, how a sample relates to the population it is drawn from, why the central limit theorem is the workhorse of inference, and how likelihood turns data into a scoring rule for competing hypotheses.

Each tutorial is self-contained and written so that a reader can land on any one page and understand the topic without prior context. Code examples use R throughout, with simulated data whenever real datasets would obscure the concept under discussion.

What you will learn

  • Scales of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio) and how they constrain the statistics you can compute
  • The distinction between a population parameter and a sample estimate, and why this matters for interpretation
  • How sampling distributions arise and why they are more fundamental than the distribution of the raw data
  • The central limit theorem and its practical implications for inference on means
  • Point estimation, unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency, and the standard error
  • Maximum likelihood estimation as a general principle for fitting models
  • Sufficient statistics and information in a sample (Fisher information)

Suggested order

Readers new to statistics may want to proceed in the order given in the sidebar; readers reviewing for a specific concept can jump directly to the relevant tutorial.
