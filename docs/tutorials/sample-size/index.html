Sample Size & Power

Power analysis and sample size planning for every common design: means, proportions, correlations, regression, survival, and equivalence/non-inferiority

Nothing is more wasteful in applied research than a study that is too small to answer its question or too large to justify its cost. Power analysis is the discipline of planning study size so that the probability of detecting a real effect is acceptably high, given a realistic expectation of what that effect will be.

This area walks through the four quantities that define every power calculation – effect size, significance level, power, and sample size – and shows how to compute any one of them given the other three, for every design a reader is likely to encounter. Practical guidance is given on choosing effect sizes from prior literature, pilot data, or minimum clinically important differences.

Designs covered

  • One-sample and two-sample tests of means (t-tests), with equal and unequal variances
  • Paired-sample tests and within-subject designs
  • One-way, factorial, and repeated-measures ANOVA
  • Tests of one and two proportions, with continuity correction and exact methods
  • Chi-squared tests of independence with arbitrary table dimensions
  • Tests for correlation coefficients
  • Sample size for linear, logistic, and Cox proportional hazards regression
  • Survival analysis: log-rank test, with accrual and follow-up separately specified
  • Equivalence and non-inferiority trials: TOST, symmetric and asymmetric margins
  • Cluster-randomised trials: design effect, ICC, and average cluster size
  • Diagnostic accuracy studies: sensitivity, specificity, ROC AUC

R packages

Tutorials use pwr, pwrss, WebPower, longpower, samplesize, Hmisc, and gsDesign as appropriate. The G*Power software is cross-referenced where it offers a useful visualisation or calculation.
